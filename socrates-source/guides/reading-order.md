# Reading Orders for the Socratic Corpus

This guide offers three ways to approach the 33 primary source texts in this corpus: by the internal dramatic timeline of Socrates' life, by the estimated order of composition, and by philosophical theme. Each ordering illuminates different aspects of the texts.

All texts in this corpus are in the original Ancient Greek. The reading orders below are useful whether you are reading the Greek directly, working with translations alongside the corpus, or feeding texts to an LLM for analysis.

---

## 2. Composition Chronology

This ordering reflects the scholarly consensus on when Plato likely wrote the dialogues, based on stylometric analysis, cross-references, and philosophical development. Xenophon's and Aristophanes' works are placed by their estimated dates of composition.

The grouping into Early, Middle, and Late periods is a standard scholarly convention (see `socratic-problem.md` for discussion of what these categories mean and their limitations).

### Early Period (c. 399-380 BCE)

These dialogues are widely considered closest to the historical Socrates. They are typically short, aporetic (ending without a definitive conclusion), and focus on "What is X?" questions about virtue.

## 3. Thematic Groupings

This ordering groups the texts by their primary philosophical concerns. Many dialogues address multiple themes; each appears under its most prominent topic.

### What Is Virtue?

The core Socratic question. These dialogues each take up a specific virtue and attempt to define it, typically ending in aporia (a productive impasse).

| Euthyphro | Plato | `plato/euthyphro/` | Piety (hosion) |

## Recommendations for First-Time Readers

If you are encountering these texts for the first time, the following sequence provides a manageable entry point:

1. **Apology** (Plato) -- Socrates explains himself at trial
2. **Euthyphro** -- A short, accessible example of Socratic questioning
3. **Crito** -- Socrates on law and obligation
4. **Meno** -- The theory of recollection and whether virtue can be taught
5. **Symposium** (Plato) -- The nature of love; Socrates as a character
6. **Phaedo** -- The death of Socrates and arguments for immortality
7. **Republic** -- The culminating work of Platonic political philosophy
8. **Clouds** (Aristophanes) -- The comic counterpoint to the philosophical portrait

From there, pursue any of the three orderings above according to your interest.
