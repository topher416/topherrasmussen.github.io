# The Socratic Problem

## Why We Cannot Be Sure What Socrates Actually Said

Socrates wrote nothing. Everything we know about him comes from other people's accounts, written after the fact, shaped by their own purposes and literary conventions. This fundamental gap between the historical person and the literary character is what scholars call "the Socratic problem."

The problem is not merely that our sources are incomplete. It is that every surviving portrait of Socrates is the work of a writer with a distinct agenda. Plato was a philosopher building a system. Xenophon was a practical man defending a mentor's reputation. Aristophanes was a comedian getting laughs. Each created a Socrates who served his own ends.

This corpus contains all the primary source texts in which Socrates appears as a character, in the original Ancient Greek. Understanding the differences between these sources is essential for reading them well -- and for using them responsibly with modern language models.

## The Three Portraits

### Plato's Socrates: The Philosopher

Plato (c. 428-348 BCE) is by far the most important source. Twenty-eight of the thirty-three texts in this corpus are attributed to him. But the relationship between Plato's "Socrates" and the historical Socrates is deeply uncertain.

In the earlier dialogues, Plato's Socrates is recognizably a specific kind of thinker: he claims to know nothing, he asks relentless questions, he deflates pretension, he argues that virtue is knowledge and that no one does wrong willingly. He practices *elenchus* -- cross-examination -- and typically leaves his interlocutors (and the reader) in a state of aporia, a productive impasse where previous certainties have been dismantled but no positive doctrine has been established.

By the middle and later dialogues, this figure transforms. The Socrates of the *Republic* confidently builds an elaborate political and metaphysical theory. The Socrates of the *Phaedo* argues at length for the immortality of the soul and the existence of a separate realm of Forms. The Socrates of the *Symposium* delivers a mystical account of love as a ladder to transcendent Beauty.

Scholars widely believe that the earlier dialogues preserve more of the historical Socrates' actual method and commitments, while the later works increasingly use "Socrates" as a mouthpiece for Plato's own developing philosophy. But where exactly to draw that line is a matter of perennial debate. There is no external test we can apply. We cannot interview Socrates. We cannot ask Plato when he stopped reporting and started inventing.

What we can say is that Plato's Socrates is a literary creation of extraordinary power and consistency, even as the philosophical content evolves. Reading the dialogues in estimated order of composition (see `reading-order.md`) makes the evolution visible.

### Xenophon's Socrates: The Practical Man

Xenophon (c. 430-354 BCE) was an Athenian soldier, historian, and gentleman farmer who studied with Socrates in his youth. Four of his works feature Socrates and are included in this corpus:

- **Memorabilia** (*Apomnemoneumata*): A collection of Socratic conversations organized thematically, explicitly written to defend Socrates against the charges that led to his execution.
- **Apology** (*Apologia Sokratous*): A short account of Socrates' defense at trial, presented as secondhand report.
- **Symposium** (*Symposion*): An account of a banquet where Socrates discusses love and virtue.
- **Oeconomicus** (*Oikonomikos*): A dialogue on household management, farming, and the education of a wife.

Xenophon's Socrates is recognizably the same person as Plato's -- he questions people, he cares about virtue, he is indifferent to money and pleasure -- but the emphasis is strikingly different. Where Plato's Socrates pursues abstract definitions and dismantles conventional wisdom, Xenophon's Socrates gives practical advice. He tells people how to manage their households, how to choose friends, how to be good soldiers. He is sensible, pious, and moderate.

Some scholars have dismissed Xenophon as philosophically unsophisticated, a writer who missed the point of Socrates' teaching. Others argue that Xenophon preserves aspects of Socrates that Plato, driven by his own philosophical ambitions, distorted or ignored. The truth may be that neither portrait is "wrong" -- they simply reflect what each writer found most important.

For corpus users, the key point is this: Xenophon's Socrates and Plato's Socrates sometimes agree and sometimes diverge. Where they agree (the importance of self-knowledge, the connection between virtue and happiness, the value of philosophical conversation), we may have our strongest evidence for the historical Socrates. Where they diverge, we are likely seeing the imprint of each author's own concerns.

### Aristophanes' Socrates: The Comic Caricature

Aristophanes (c. 446-386 BCE) was the greatest poet of Athenian Old Comedy. His play *Clouds* (*Nephelai*), produced in 423 BCE, is the earliest surviving depiction of Socrates -- written when Socrates was about forty-seven years old and very much alive.

The Socrates of the *Clouds* is almost unrecognizable from Plato's and Xenophon's portraits. He runs a "Thinkery" (*phrontisterion*) where he hangs in a basket to study the heavens, teaches that Zeus does not exist, and instructs students in how to make the weaker argument defeat the stronger. He is presented as a combination of natural philosopher (studying clouds, air, and cosmic vortices) and rhetorical trickster -- essentially a sophist.

Plato himself, in the *Apology*, has Socrates address the *Clouds* directly, arguing that Aristophanes' portrayal is a longstanding source of prejudice against him. Whether Aristophanes intended real malice or was simply following the conventions of comic exaggeration is debated. What is clear is that the *Clouds* tells us something about how Socrates was perceived by at least some Athenians in the 420s: as a strange, impious intellectual associated with the new learning.

The *Clouds* in this corpus is the revised version that survives, not the original produced in 423. The revision probably dates to c. 418-416 BCE and was apparently never staged.

## Early, Middle, and Late Plato

Scholars conventionally divide Plato's dialogues into three (sometimes four) groups based on their estimated order of composition. This framework is important because it tracks a dramatic evolution in what "Socrates" says and does across the corpus.

### Early Dialogues (c. 399-380 BCE)

**Typical works**: Apology, Crito, Euthyphro, Laches, Charmides, Lysis, Hippias Minor, Hippias Major, Ion, Protagoras, Euthydemus

**Characteristics**:
- Socrates professes ignorance and seeks definitions
- Dialogues are typically aporetic: they end without a positive conclusion
- The method is *elenchus*: Socrates questions an interlocutor's beliefs until contradictions emerge
- No Theory of Forms, no elaborate metaphysics
- Positive theses are few but consistent: virtue is knowledge, no one errs willingly, the virtues are unified

**Why they matter**: Scholars generally consider the early dialogues the closest window onto the historical Socrates' actual philosophical practice. The Socrates depicted here is a questioner, not a system-builder.

### Middle Dialogues (c. 380-360 BCE)

**Typical works**: Phaedo, Symposium, Republic, Phaedrus

**Characteristics**:
- Socrates remains the main speaker but now advances positive theories
- The Theory of Forms appears: abstract, perfect entities (Justice itself, Beauty itself) that particular things participate in
- The soul has a complex structure (the tripartite soul of *Republic* Book IV)
- Extended myths and images (the allegory of the cave, the allegory of the charioteer)
- Political theory: the philosopher-king, the just city

**Why they matter**: This is where Plato's own philosophy takes center stage. Most scholars believe the Theory of Forms, the tripartite soul, and the philosopher-king are Plato's ideas, not things the historical Socrates taught. "Socrates" has become a literary vehicle.

### Late Dialogues (c. 360-347 BCE)

**Typical works**: Parmenides, Theaetetus, Sophist, Statesman, Philebus, Timaeus, Critias

**Characteristics**:
- Socrates is increasingly displaced as the main speaker (the Eleatic Stranger leads in *Sophist* and *Statesman*; Timaeus leads in *Timaeus*)
- Plato subjects his own earlier theories to rigorous criticism (the self-critique of the Theory of Forms in *Parmenides*)
- Style becomes more technical and less dramatic
- Stylometric analysis (statistical study of word frequencies, sentence patterns, and prose rhythm) confirms these dialogues cluster together and differ from the early works

**Why they matter**: The late dialogues show Plato as a self-critical thinker who does not treat his own earlier positions as sacrosanct. They also show how the character of "Socrates" has become less necessary to the philosophical project.

### Limitations of this Framework

The Early-Middle-Late scheme is useful but imperfect:

- The boundaries are porous and disputed. Scholars disagree about where individual dialogues fall (the *Gorgias*, *Meno*, and *Cratylus* are variously placed as late-early or transitional).
- Stylometry provides relative ordering but cannot give absolute dates.
- Plato may have revised earlier works, so the version we read may not match the original composition date.
- The assumption that Plato moved linearly from "reporting Socrates" to "using Socrates as a mouthpiece" may be too simple. Plato was a literary artist who may have adopted different approaches for different purposes at any point in his career.

## Why This Matters for LLM Interactions

When using this corpus with large language models, the Socratic problem creates specific risks and opportunities.

### Risks

**Conflation of sources.** A model trained on all 33 texts without guidance may blend Plato's metaphysical Socrates with Xenophon's practical Socrates and Aristophanes' comic Socrates into a single incoherent figure. If asked "What did Socrates believe?", it may produce a composite that no ancient source actually presents.

**Treating Plato's words as Socrates' words.** The single most common error is to attribute the Theory of Forms, the tripartite soul, or the philosopher-king to Socrates rather than to Plato. A model may do this if it takes the dialogue form at face value, treating every speech by the character "Socrates" as a report of the historical person's views.

**Ignoring dramatic context.** The dialogues are literary works, not transcripts. Socrates' statements are shaped by who he is talking to, what the dramatic situation is, and what effect Plato wants to create. A model that extracts propositions without attending to context will miss the philosophical point.

### Opportunities

**Source comparison.** LLMs can rapidly compare how the same topic is treated across sources. Asking a model to compare Plato's and Xenophon's accounts of the trial, or Plato's and Aristophanes' depictions of Socrates' relationship to natural philosophy, can surface genuine scholarly questions.

**Tracking philosophical evolution.** By feeding the model texts in compositional order and asking it to identify shifts in vocabulary, argument structure, or the role of the character Socrates, you can use LLMs as a tool for exploring the development of Plato's thought.

**Greek language analysis.** Because this corpus provides the original Greek, LLMs with strong multilingual capabilities can analyze philosophical terminology directly -- tracking how terms like *arete* (excellence/virtue), *episteme* (knowledge), *doxa* (opinion/belief), and *eidos* (form/idea) are used across different texts and authors.

### Best Practices

1. **Always specify which author and text you are asking about.** Instead of "What did Socrates think about justice?", ask "In Plato's *Republic*, how does the character Socrates define justice?"
2. **Distinguish the character from the person.** Use "Plato's Socrates" rather than "Socrates" when discussing Platonic texts.
3. **Specify the period.** If your question concerns a specific stage of Plato's thought, indicate whether you are asking about an early, middle, or late dialogue.
4. **Cross-reference sources.** When a topic appears in both Plato and Xenophon, ask the model to compare rather than synthesize.
5. **Attend to what the Greek actually says.** When a philosophical distinction seems important, ask the model to examine the original Greek terminology rather than relying on traditional translations.
