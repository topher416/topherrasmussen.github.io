# Apology 38a — Translation and Commentary

**Source:** Plato, *Apology of Socrates* (Ἀπολογία Σωκράτους)
**Edition:** Burnet, *Platonis Opera*, OCT 1900–1907 (via Perseus Digital Library, CC BY-SA 3.0 US)
**Stephanus reference:** 38a

## Greek Text

ἡσυχίαν ἄγειν, οὐ πείσεσθέ μοι ὡς εἰρωνευομένῳ· ἐάντʼ αὖ λέγω ὅτι καὶ
τυγχάνει μέγιστον ἀγαθὸν ὂν ἀνθρώπῳ τοῦτο, ἑκάστης ἡμέρας περὶ ἀρετῆς τοὺς
λόγους ποιεῖσθαι καὶ τῶν ἄλλων περὶ ὧν ὑμεῖς ἐμοῦ ἀκούετε διαλεγομένου καὶ
ἐμαυτὸν καὶ ἄλλους ἐξετάζοντος, ὁ δὲ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ,
ταῦτα δʼ ἔτι ἧττον πείσεσθέ μοι λέγοντι. τὰ δὲ ἔχει μὲν οὕτως, ὡς ἐγώ φημι,
ὦ ἄνδρες, πείθειν δὲ οὐ ῥᾴδιον.

## Translation

"…to keep quiet, you will not believe me, thinking I am being ironic. And if on
the other hand I say that this happens to be the greatest good for a human
being—to make arguments every day about virtue and about the other things you
hear me discussing, examining both myself and others—and that **the unexamined
life is not worth living for a human being**, you will believe me even less when
I say these things. Yet the matter stands as I say, men of Athens, though it is
not easy to persuade you of it."

## Why This Passage Matters

### 1. The most famous sentence in Western philosophy

"The unexamined life is not worth living" (ὁ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς
ἀνθρώπῳ) is uttered not as an abstract aphorism but as a concrete defense of
Socrates' own life—at a moment when that life is at stake. He has just been
found guilty and is now addressing the penalty phase.

### 2. The dramatic context

Socrates is explaining why he cannot accept exile with a promise to stop
philosophizing. He presents the jury with a dilemma: if he says the god forbids
him to be silent, they will think he is being ironic (εἰρωνευομένῳ); if he says
daily philosophical conversation is the greatest human good, they will believe
him even less. He knows his honesty will cost him his life—and says it anyway.

### 3. Philosophy as daily practice

The phrase ἑκάστης ἡμέρας ("every day") is easily overlooked but essential.
Socrates is not describing philosophy as an occasional intellectual exercise
but as a way of life—daily conversation about virtue (ἀρετή), daily
self-examination (ἐμαυτὸν ἐξετάζοντος). This grounds the Socratic method not
as a technique but as an ethical commitment.

### 4. The word ἀνεξέταστος

The alpha-privative form of ἐξετάζω ("to examine, to test") is striking. It is
the same verb Socrates uses throughout the Apology for his interrogation of
Athenian politicians, poets, and craftsmen. The "unexamined life" is literally
the "untested" life—the life that has not been subjected to the kind of
cross-examination Socrates was condemned for practicing.

### 5. Connection to the trial tetralogy

This passage links the Apology to the broader dramatic sequence: Euthyphro →
Apology → Crito → Phaedo. The claim that the examined life is the only life
worth living becomes the philosophical thread connecting Socrates' pre-trial
piety questioning (Euthyphro), his refusal to escape (Crito), and his calm
acceptance of death (Phaedo).
