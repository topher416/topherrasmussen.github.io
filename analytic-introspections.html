Introduction

I am not a scientist.

A scientist would hypothesize and experiment and verify and analyze and eventually prove something. A scientist's thinking would be methodical and rigorous and sharp.

Introspection is a problematic scientific apparatus. Wolfgang KÃ¶hler explains its flaw: "as a procedure, introspection lacks the chief methodological virtue of work in physics: a position of the observer outside the system which he observes. Introspecting and its objects are facts within the same system, and the chances that the former leave the latter undisturbed are exceedingly small."

The writings assembled here may not be valuable as scientific data, but the process of collecting introspections over the course of the project had a significant impact on my inner experience, which is something you'll just have to take my word on.

Over the past two semesters I have continually attempted to capture what it feels like to be a 28-year-old actor/psychology student writing an undergraduate thesis paper about acting and psychology. In doing so, my hope is that I've written an undergraduate thesis paper about acting and psychology.

Methodology and Style

"Whereas incorrect Activity causes contraction in our body and soul, correct Activity frees us and leads to expansion. The actor must discriminate between the two kinds of Activity in himself, and must try always to keep to the right one." Michael Chekhov, On the Technique of Acting

In developing this project, writing began to pour out of me. I began with what felt like academic formality, making sweeping claims about Freud and Stanislavski, defending my argument and imagining conclusions. As I continued writing and reading and thinking, the formality and argument began to feel hollow and distant, so I let it fall away. Allowing myself to write without knowing where I was headed proved beneficial- my explorations deepened, my reflective thinking gained sharpness, and my curiosity took the driver's seat. Continued research and writing became not only fruitful, but pleasurable. Framed the way I framed it to myself, the writing process is unrestrained- offered room to expand, to shift, to meander.

The pursuit of my goal to become a better actor began by gathering stacks of ideas and approaches so that I could write with some authority on Acting and Psychology, so that I could be more deeply informed on the ways that others have thought about the intersection between the two fields. Reading and thinking about the topics from an objective perspective was interesting and in some way I developed a position in the ongoing conversation, allying myself generally with Michael Chekhov and C.G. Jung.

As time passed and the audition approached, my writing strayed from a focus on what others have said and what I want to say about acting and psychology and toward my subjective experience, which felt more relevant than theory or argument, because I was experiencing the intersection of acting and psychology. I started using writing as a way to unpack my anxiety and discovered that the attitude with which I approached my writing (which is the same as my thinking or is at least a part of it) had a direct impact on my experience of performing. The more I indulged my anxiety as I wrote, the more anxious my performance became. I corrected my approach and did less, wrote less, and the outcome was a calmer, more present performance.

In order to become better, I needed to take stock- to find out where I am now, which cannot be separated from what led me to here. So, I began thinking backward. I also started paying closer attention to my dreams, the contents of which are an amalgamation of imagining, remembering, and experiencing. Then I paid less attention to my dreams because I had to get to work and I did not have 30 minutes to write longhand in a dream journal but I still tried to seek meaning in my dreaming.

Then, after the audition, after being cast, my thinking (and my writing, which became more sparse) turned further inward. I felt it important to uncover what kind of actor I am- and in the digging, I could not distinguish between what kind of actor I am and what kind of person I am.

At one point, this writing was intended to precede a live performance- it would be one part of a theatrical experience that would culminate in total embodied enactment.

Then, SARS-CoV-2 turned the world upside-down and audience members became a threat to each other just by sitting in proximity and theatres worldwide shut their doors and being an actor and being a person became more confusing than it was before.

These pieces of writing land somewhere in the overlap between autobiographical narrative, process journal, and memoir. My hope is that the reader will engage with my confusions and excitements and will not be alienated.

Preface

On a beach in Southern California when I was four years old I remember watching the tide recede around my ankles and then a wave slammed into me suddenly and it was so loud and the wind was knocked out of me and I could only see churning water and sand and I couldn't breathe and I didn't know what was happening but I thought I was dying.

As the water settled and I found my hands and knees I was coughing and saltwater was burning my eyes and I could not catch my breath and my mouth was full of sand and seawater and it tasted painfully bitter. I don't remember if it was my dad or my mom but somebody scooped me up and wrapped me in a towel and I shivered there on their lap.

Whenever we returned to the beach as I got older I would watch people swimming out there past the waves with deep confusion.

My uncle relentlessly coaxed me back into the water and taunted me with how unscary it was to him but he was so much bigger than me and I didn't think he understood what had happened and what could happen.

Later I learned that I could confront oncoming waves directly, dipping beneath the crest of the billow and emerging on the other side and looking back to watch it crash. I felt powerful floating beyond the violent edge.

Then when I was thirteen my uncle handed me a wetsuit and snorkelling gear and we went out into the water but not very far and when I looked beneath the surface a mass of seaweed undulated and it seemed alive and immense and my heart was pounding and I jolted back and rushed to the shore and sat and shivered.

When standing in front of an audience, their collected attention assumes an oceanic form, sending waves of energy into and through me.

The following writing is itself a performance, an accounting of my subjective emotional processes, excitements, and anxieties over the course of several months. The initial intent behind the project was to investigate overlaps between acting theory and psychoanalytic theory as I prepare to play Freddie in Sarah Ruhl's How to Transcend a Happy Marriage at Salt Lake Acting Company. Then, a global pandemic struck. Then, the writing itself became the point.

On many levels, acting and psychoanalysis both concern the inner world of the individual. Thoughts and feelings are the raw material used in both processes: here are some of mine.

August 21, 2019 - in which the brainstorm begins

I sit facing an immense shifting mass of questions and possibilities regarding form, content, intention, and process.

There are countless intersections between acting and psychology. In order to progress toward a cogent thesis (or specific topic, for that matter) it is imperative that I make clear what sort of acting I wish to dissect, and in which context. I have no idea where to begin.

As a spiritually frustrated teenager, I found respite in acting classes. I wasn't offered any answers to deep questions about my existence, but in sussing out the meaning of a line and imagining circumstances outside my personal experience I felt my angst subsiding and my curiosity awakening.

There is a difference between the sensation of reading a play and the sensation of reading a script that contains a character that you as the actor are committed to playing. To the actor, each line, each word, each punctuation mark, even each space between lines- every symbol or scrap of information relevant to the present role ceases to be inert ink on a page when a performance looms.

The craft of acting has been described and taught and analyzed and deconstructed and restructured and mastered and butchered many times by actors, teachers, directors, critics, and theorists for hundreds of years. I don't know that there is anything new I can offer to the discussion.

Konstantin Stanislavski is generally considered the grandfather of modern acting theory. The instigating force behind his approach is psychology and affective memory.1

I have been thinking seriously about what I most want to do for a long time. In many ways, I am doing it. I am doing it through my work at Salt Lake Acting Company2; through my relationship with my girlfriend, Cassie, who is an actor of an incredibly high caliber; through my study of psychology and continual discovery of parallels to the craft of acting; through my continued commitment to supporting the development and production of brave contemporary theatre.

So- what will my capstone project look like? There are a number of swirling possibilities- some look like a performance, some look like a play that I direct, some that I write, some look like a practical guide for actors, some look like a series of acting exercises. Very few look like a traditional academic research paper.

In the end, my goal is to become a better actor.

September 2, 2019 - in which it is revealed just how little I know about psychology

I have begun to loathe psychology. Not through my own thinking about it- I enjoyed reading about Freud's deeply beautiful, totally misguided ideas and tragic, hilarious life story3- but as I read and absorb acting theory, I start to resent the intent behind psychological study and the relentless rationalism of the scientific method. In the process of seeking wisdom about the ways theatre-makers move audiences, I find myself increasingly distanced from the rationally data-driven, from the scientific particulars, from attempts to nail down concrete facts about the thinking mind.

I was surprised to learn about how desperate Freud was to make a scientific discovery that would make him famous. He hoped to become a Newton or a Darwin. He was obsessive about it, surrounding himself with thinkers he viewed as influential, making them his closest friends until they betrayed him and became his mortal enemies. The two-page biography in Theories of Personality describes a paranoid person who seems like a shoo-in for bipolar, if not full-on borderline personality disorder4. He continually painted himself into corners by making sweeping claims and had difficulty amending his theories as his life went on.

When interviewed by Paula Vogel in BOMB Magazine, contemporary playwright Sarah Ruhl nudged the theatre field away from Freud's influence: "I think our generation has to look at Freud and Freud's impact, and many of us say, Oh, maybe Freud didn't have it right. Something that he was right about he got from literature: the Oedipal complex, from the Greeks. So maybe we ought to go back to the Greeks instead of back to Freud on the Greeks."

Ruhl goes back to the Greeks, but not through the conventional avenues that so frequently make Ancient Greek theatre dusty and boring. Her instinct is to follow Ovid instead of Aristotle, trading well-worn plot arcs for magical, simple moments of transformation. In Melancholy Play, for example, a woman gets so depressed that she becomes an almond.

Hers is nowhere near the only approach to making theatre, nor anywhere near the most common. Many would argue that a play requires realistic psychology, that to tell an effective story the characters and events must be, above all, believable. Ruhl resists the rational, realistic urge. She distills her approach in a profile from the New Yorker: "If you excavate people's subjectivity and how they view the world emotionally, you don't get realism."

It seemed, to me, that the study of psychology would open a window into the human mind that would benefit my craft as an actor. If I could amass encyclopedic knowledge of the psyche, I would gain the key to unlocking the secrets of characters I previously couldn't even see. I thought I had a path forward, a road toward mastery that would lead me in the right direction.

As I see it today, the trajectory I had imagined was woefully naive.5 Getting an analytical perspective on the history of psychological practice will be interesting, sure, but studying the mind and acting require radically different skill sets.

I hope to continue running into these walls. I arrive at a conclusion and then I read something deeper, something outside, and I realize how little I understood. As it seems to me today, psychology is so preoccupied with the thinking mind that in considering the way human beings function in the world it neglects the journey of the heart.

I stand in agreement with Ruhl: "Psychological realism makes emotions so rational, so explained, that they don't feel like emotions to me."

How could I have been so blind?

September 8, 2019 - changing my mind again [manic episode]

Last night I lay awake, my mind racing, making connections, visualizing the springtime and my project reaching completion, hallucinating my victory over myself, proving to the world that I'm not a fuckup who couldn't keep a job or earn a degree. I ordered stacks of books, more than my shelves have room for, more than I will be able to read, more than made financial sense.6 I jumped far ahead of myself, spending hours poring over articles about Michael Chekhov and the embodied imagination, Mac Wellman essays on non-Euclidean character and the deficiencies of the American well-made play, sucking down information like a vacuum cleaner, like a black hole. Considering how little sleep I was getting at the time, I am unsure of the degree to which I retained any of the ideas.

At the same time, I took another step into the history of psychoanalysis. I discovered that many of Freud's contemporaries were frustrated with his methods. In recounting Alfred Adler's journey away from Freud, the authors of Theories of Personality seemed to anticipate how distanced from Freud I had been feeling. Adler's radically different theory fell into place and I found myself nodding, then dissociating, then reevaluating my childhood, and finally sitting in the realization that an inflated sense of inferiority hid beneath the development of my personality.

Before my encounter with Adler, I was inflamed with the notion of my thesis project. I found myself swept away with images of myself creating the Most Beautiful And Insightful And Moving Thesis Project Ever, gaining complete mastery of the work of every acting theorist and psychologist that's ever lived, solving acting, solving psychology, and ascending into perfect glory.

According to Adler, "maladjusted people set extravagant goals as an overcompensation for exaggerated feelings of inferiority."

People who feel inferior are motivated to prove their superiority, which decreases their ability to cultivate GemeinschaftsgefÃ¼hl, a delightfully German word that encapsulates a feeling of oneness with their community. If your goal is to prove you're better than others, their successes feel like a threat.

Adler says that children naturally feel inferior. To a child, an adult is a giant. When I was small, my father's frustrations and anxieties overwhelmed me. When he was angry he was immense and I was very afraid. I didn't know how to protect myself or my sisters or my mother. He was so much bigger than me.

The impulse to self-diagnose is both unwise and unavoidable. Freud himself was guilty of it, spending inordinate amounts of time attempting to untangle his own mind. My textbook quotes him as saying, "the chief patient I am preoccupied with is myself."

The fifth edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders lists the symptoms of a manic episode as: "elevated mood, inflated self-esteem, decreased need for sleep, racing thoughts, difficulty maintaining attention, increase in goal-directed activity, and excessive involvement in pleasurable activities." To qualify as a manic episode, three of these symptoms must be present. My recent flurry of activity checks six out of seven symptoms.

Adlerian analysis places emphasis on the trajectory of the individual, considering the individual's vision of themselves in the future as the primary motivator of behavior. This teleological perspective on human striving aligns directly with Stanislavski's conception of the super-objective.7 Adler calls it the individual's Fictional Final Goal.

Instead of attempting to portray emotional states directly, Stanislavski asks the actor to consider what the character wants and how they go about getting it.

Though Lee Strasberg is at minimum two degrees of separation from Stanislavski himself, he articulates core aspects of Stanislavski's philosophy clearly:

The aim of the modern method is to turn the actor away from a concern with what he, the actor, is doing, thus ridding him of his fundamental self-consciousness; and to create for him those proper elements of what the character is doing which become the impulses for behavior. The actor thus becomes concerned with acting the causes, and not the results, permitting the latter to occur as they do in life.

I loved reading about Adler's flexible and affable approach to talking therapy. Instead of sitting behind the patient and making declarative observations, he'd engage face-to-face with each patient, treating them as an individual, investing in their well-being. Rather than their past, he'd seek to understand their hopes and dreams and goals for the future. Adler saw the individual for their potential, where Freud seemed more interested in digging up deep dark urges and resentments from early childhood.8

Despite my initial sense of discouragement about my mania and my inferiority complex, I feel prepared to continue my research and study. I can see a grounded, healthy path forward, one in which I am as interested in the success of those around me as I am in my own achievement.

September 15, 2019 - in which I doubt my approach

As I stirred from an unplanned nap this afternoon I felt a wave of panic as my to-do list came rushing back to me. Before I fell asleep I had been watching youtube videos on the Jungian individuation process, the Anima and Animus, Jung's tower at Bollingen, and the esoteric mandalas he carved into a stone cube there.

I grazed the surface of a vivid dream and watched myself rushing down the massive set of stairs that leads from the GT building to the rest of campus, my feet barely touching each step as I rapidly descend.

The fields of psychology and acting theory have so much in common that even where they intersect, they do so broadly. There are infinite parallels- historically, methodologically, culturally. They have so much in common that in some ways it is harder to find differences than similarities.

Michael Chekhov mirrors Jung in countless ways. Like Jung, he speaks of the occult, the woo, the archetypal9, and acknowledges the energetic fields10 that lie outside the brain. His approach extends into what Jung calls the collective unconscious, beyond the reach of Stanislavski's psychological system.

"Affective memory" as a technique for manifesting psychologically realistic acting has its strengths.11 Each actor must bring a part of themselves to the role- must consider the character's worldview, perceive their hopes, acknowledge their traumas, and find ways to justify their actions, folding the actor's personal experiences into their interpretation. That said, affective memory has limitations.

Comparative religion contributed significantly to Jung's discovery of the collective unconscious and enabled him to suss out universal archetypal forms in disparate cultures. In some ways, I hope to treat acting theory and psychological theory in the same way- comparing, finding commonalities, and uncovering archetypal forms.12

In thinking about this project and talking about it with Cassie it has become clear that I don't need to solve psychology or solve acting. Despite this acknowledgment, I continue to encounter a relentless feeling that I need to solve myself. Jung's path toward individuation feels like a place to start. But- fuck. Solve myself? That's a lifelong process. How is this project even related to that? Why can't this just be a research paper? Isn't this just an undergraduate degree?

Why does it feel like I'm on the verge of something big? Why has it always felt that way? Will I ever arrive? Am I making any progress at all?

September 20, 2019 - questions regarding praxis

Auditions for Sarah Ruhl's How to Transcend a Happy Marriage at Salt Lake Acting Company are in four days. I have not read the play yet, but word in the community is that it's risky, it's funny, and it's exciting. According to an interview I found, Ruhl wrote the play after her own experience at a party in which she met a polyamorous triad. She was fascinated by the inner lives of the people involved.

I have developed a habit of not reading the play I'm auditioning for beforehand. I came to this approach after performing in Lauren Gunderson's I and You at SLAC a couple years ago. A friend and I both auditioned for the show but neither of us read the play and we both got callbacks. At the callback, everyone who had read the play was visibly anxious because the material deals with big emotional territory (mortality, terminal illness, friendship, isolation). My friend and I remained calm because we didn't know what the play was about. The director later told me that he cast us in part because we didn't have presuppositions- there were no layers to peel away.

My strategy for auditions has become to not prepare at all. I show up, I read the audition scenes, and I see what happens. This method is particularly effective when auditioning for directors I know well and plays that seem like they'd be a good fit for me. Now I'm about to audition for How to Transcend and I still don't know what it's about beyond that brief mention from Ruhl's interview.

Here's what I do know: I've wanted to work with Keven Myhre (the director) for years. He's an incredibly talented actor and director. I know the SLAC artistic staff trusts my abilities since I've been a company member for two seasons. I'm familiar with Ruhl's style and sensibilities. The world of the play involves sexuality and nudity which I'm comfortable with.

This is enough context to walk into an audition room.

What I can't ignore, though, is the apprehension I feel about possibly being cast. It's not a fear of the work itself- I love being on stage, I love performing, I love the collaborative environment of rehearsal. What terrifies me is the scrutiny. Once cast, I become the subject of observation. The director watches, the other actors watch, and eventually the audience watches. All those eyes create pressure, expectation, judgment.

There's a psychological principle called the "social facilitation effect" which suggests that the presence of others improves performance on simple or well-practiced tasks but impairs performance on complex or new tasks. Auditions typically involve simple tasks- read these sides, show us your interpretation, give us a sense of who you are. But rehearsals and performances involve complex tasks that require sustained concentration, emotional availability, physical presence, and intellectual engagement.

The difference between auditioning and performing is like the difference between introducing yourself at a party and having an intimate conversation. One is surface-level and manageable; the other requires vulnerability and sustained attention.

I'm scared of the vulnerability.

October 1, 2019 - interlude: on giftedness

When I was eight years old, I took a test that determined I was "gifted." This label followed me through school, providing me with advanced classes, enrichment opportunities, and a subtle but persistent message: you are special, you are capable of more than others, you have potential.

The "gifted" label is a double-edged sword. On one side, it opens doors and creates opportunities. On the other side, it establishes expectations that become internalized as obligations. If you're gifted, you should excel. If you're special, you should prove it constantly.

Carol Dweck's research on mindset distinguishes between a "fixed mindset" (believing that abilities are innate and unchangeable) and a "growth mindset" (believing that abilities can be developed through effort and practice). The gifted label tends to cultivate a fixed mindset. If you're told from a young age that you're smart or talented, you begin to see these qualities as inherent rather than developed. This creates a fear of failure because failure suggests that maybe you weren't actually gifted after all.

Acting requires a growth mindset. Every role is different, every director has different expectations, every script presents new challenges. An actor can't rely on innate talent alone- they must be willing to experiment, fail, adjust, and try again. But the specter of giftedness lingers, whispering that if I were truly talented, this would be easier.

Lewis Hyde's The Gift explores the concept of talent as a gift that must be given away rather than hoarded. He argues that creative work functions in a gift economy rather than a market economy- the more you give, the more you receive. But this requires letting go of ownership, letting go of the need to prove your worth through your work.

Maybe the real challenge isn't proving I'm gifted. Maybe the challenge is giving the gift away.

October 2, 2019 - turning toward the wave

In my preface, I described that childhood experience of being slammed by a wave at the beach. I've been thinking about that memory and what it represents in terms of stage fright and performance anxiety.

Michael Chekhov uses the metaphor of "radiation" to describe the energy an actor sends out into the space. He suggests that actors can consciously direct their energy outward, creating a palpable presence that affects both other actors and the audience. This concept of radiation assumes that performance isn't just about what you do but about what you emanate.

In my experience, stage fright feels like the opposite of radiation. Instead of energy flowing outward, everything contracts inward. My breath becomes shallow, my muscles tense, my awareness narrows to a pinpoint focus on my own inadequacy. The audience, rather than receiving my energy, becomes a threat to be defended against.

But what if I could turn toward the wave instead of bracing against it?

This is what I learned after that initial traumatic wave experience- that I could dive under the wave, let it pass over me, and emerge on the other side. This requires a counterintuitive response: instead of tensing up and trying to stay rigid, you have to relax and surrender to the force that's coming at you.

Applied to performance, this means acknowledging the audience's presence rather than trying to ignore it or fight against it. It means accepting the vulnerability of being watched rather than defending against it. It means trusting that if I relax into the experience, I'll emerge intact on the other side.

Chekhov again: "The actor must have absolute confidence in his creative intuition." Confidence doesn't mean certainty- it means trust. Trust that even when the wave comes, even when the audience's attention feels overwhelming, I have the capacity to stay present and let it pass through me rather than knock me down.

October 3, 2019 - lull [depressive episode]

I haven't written in a week. I've been sleeping ten, eleven, twelve hours a night and still feeling exhausted. I haven't been to the gym. I've been eating poorly. I've been avoiding social situations. I've been watching YouTube videos about acting technique and psychoanalytic theory but retaining nothing.

This isn't surprising. The manic episode I described earlier was bound to crash eventually. This is the pattern I've recognized over the past few years- a period of intense, almost frenzied activity followed by a period of lethargy and withdrawal. The DSM-5 calls this "Bipolar II" when the manic episodes are less severe (called "hypomanic episodes") and don't cause significant impairment.

I've been thinking about whether to seek treatment. Part of me worries that medication would dull the creative spark that comes with the hypomanic episodes. Another part of me recognizes that this cycle is unsustainable- I can't function effectively when I'm swinging between extremes.

Kay Redfield Jamison's An Unquiet Mind describes her own struggle with bipolar disorder and her resistance to medication. She's a psychologist who specializes in mood disorders, which gave her unique insight into her own condition but also made her particularly susceptible to the romanticization of madness that permeates artistic culture. The myth of the tortured artist suggests that mental illness and creativity are inextricably linked, that treating the illness would eliminate the creativity.

But Jamison eventually came to understand that untreated bipolar disorder doesn't enhance creativity- it disrupts it. The depressive episodes are characterized by an inability to create anything at all. The manic episodes produce a volume of work, but much of it is scattered and unfocused. Stability, it turns out, is more conducive to sustained creative work than chaos.

Still, I'm not ready to seek treatment yet. Part of this is denial. Part of it is fear. Part of it is the fact that I'm about to audition for a play and I don't want to introduce any new variables into my psychological state.

For now, I'm just trying to get through each day.

October 6, 2019 - the apprehension returns

The audition is tomorrow. I still haven't read the play. I've done no preparation. This isn't strategic laziness- it's anxiety avoidance. If I read the play and discover that I want to be in it, then the stakes of the audition increase. If I want something, I can be disappointed when I don't get it. If I don't allow myself to want it in the first place, I protect myself from that potential disappointment.

This is classic avoidant attachment style. Attachment theory, originally developed by John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth, describes patterns of relating to others based on early childhood experiences with caregivers. People with avoidant attachment tend to maintain emotional distance as a defensive strategy- if you don't get close to anyone, no one can hurt you.

I can see this pattern playing out not just in relationships but in my approach to creative work. If I don't invest fully in the audition, if I don't prepare or even read the play, then I'm not really trying. And if I'm not really trying, then failure doesn't mean anything about my abilities or worth.

But this strategy has a cost. By protecting myself from potential disappointment, I also prevent myself from experiencing genuine success. A victory achieved without investment feels hollow. And more importantly, by holding back in this way, I deprive myself of the opportunity to discover what I'm actually capable of when I commit fully.

Chekhov writes: "The actor should never worry about his talent, but rather about his lack of technique, his lack of training, and his lack of understanding of the creative process."

Maybe I've been worrying about the wrong thing. Maybe the question isn't whether I have talent or whether I'll succeed at the audition. Maybe the question is whether I'm willing to fully engage with the creative process, with all its attendant risks and vulnerabilities.

Tomorrow, I'll find out.

October 10, 2019 - October 26, 2019

Although the actor feels free at rehearsals, in the sense that he is not yet responsible for the actual performance, he is nevertheless under surveillance. This can cause considerable anxiety if he feels insecure. Michael Chekhov, On the Technique of Acting

October 10, 2019 - 4:10 pm, directly following the audition

I just left the audition room and I need to write this down before the experience fades.

I arrived at the theatre fifteen minutes early and sat in my car, breathing, trying to calm my racing heart. I still hadn't read the play. I had glanced at the audition sides the night before but hadn't memorized them or thought deeply about them.

Walking into the building, I felt that familiar contraction- the sense that I was about to be evaluated, judged, found wanting. The lobby was empty except for one other actor who I recognized from previous productions. We nodded at each other but didn't speak.

October 18, 2019 - interlude: on new york, on being in your head

I've been thinking about my time living in New York, six years ago when I was 22. I moved there with dreams of making it as an actor, convinced that I just needed to be in the right place to be discovered.

I lasted nine months.

The problem wasn't lack of opportunity. I got auditions. I did showcase performances. I took classes with working actors. The problem was that I couldn't get out of my own head long enough to actually engage with any of it.

The term "self-conscious" is interesting. Literally, it means being conscious of the self- aware of yourself as an object of perception. But in common usage, it carries a negative connotation: being excessively aware of yourself, trapped in your own perspective, unable to lose yourself in the experience at hand.

Chekhov addresses this directly: "The aim of the modern method is to turn the actor away from a concern with what he, the actor, is doing, thus ridding him of his fundamental self-consciousness."

But how do you turn away from self-consciousness? You can't will yourself to stop thinking about yourself- that just creates more self-consciousness. It's like the old instruction "don't think about a white elephant"- the moment you try not to think about it, you're thinking about it.

In New York, I tried to force my way out of self-consciousness through sheer discipline and willpower. I thought that if I just worked harder, prepared more thoroughly, analyzed more deeply, I could control my way into spontaneity. This is, of course, a contradiction. Spontaneity can't be controlled. Presence can't be manufactured.

Eventually I gave up and moved back to Utah, telling myself I was taking a break but knowing, on some level, that I was running away.

And now here I am, six years later, still wrestling with the same issues. Still trying to figure out how to be present without being self-conscious. Still hoping that the next role will be the one where I finally break through whatever barrier keeps me from fully inhabiting my work.

Maybe the barrier is the hope itself. Maybe as long as I'm waiting for some future breakthrough, I'm avoiding the present moment where the actual work happens.

In her book Overcoming Stage Fright in Everyday Life, psychologist Janet Ashley distinguishes between healthy stage fright (which mobilizes energy and sharpens focus) and debilitating stage fright (which paralyzes and prevents authentic engagement). The difference lies not in the intensity of the physiological response but in how we interpret that response.

If I interpret my racing heart as a sign that I'm in danger, my body goes into full defensive mode- muscles tense, breathing becomes shallow, attention narrows. But if I interpret that same racing heart as excitement or readiness, the physiological response remains but the quality of the experience changes.

This is what I need to practice: reinterpreting my fear as excitement, my vulnerability as openness, my self-consciousness as awareness.

Rehearsals start in four weeks. Between now and then, I need to do the preparatory work- learn the lines, research the world of the play, think about Freddie's backstory and relationships.

October 28, 2019 - November 17, 2019

October 28, 2019 - purging the yuck

I've been experiencing this sensation that's hard to articulate- a kind of psychic congestion, a buildup of anxious energy that needs to be released. Writing helps, but only partially. I'll pour out pages of introspection and analysis and feel temporarily relieved, but then the congestion returns.

Stage fright isn't a cognitive issue that can be solved through better understanding. It's a somatic, embodied experience that manifests as tension, shallowness of breath, racing thoughts.

The Michael Chekhov technique emphasizes the body as the primary instrument of the actor. Rather than starting with psychological analysis (as in the Stanislavski system), Chekhov begins with physical exploration. He develops exercises based on archetypal gestures- expanding, contracting, pulling, pushing- and encourages actors to discover emotional truths through physical experimentation.

What if I approached my stage fright not as a problem to be analyzed but as a physical pattern to be transformed?

The pattern looks like this: awareness of being observed â tightening in the chest and throat â shallow breathing â narrowing of attention â increased self-consciousness â more tightening â more shallow breathing â anxiety spiral.

The Chekhov technique would suggest an intervention at the physical level. Instead of trying to change the thoughts or feelings, change the body. If I can interrupt the tightening, open the chest, deepen the breath, expand the attention- then the emotional cascade might not trigger in the same way.

I've been doing an exercise from Chekhov's book: standing in my living room, imagining I'm radiating energy outward in all directions. At first it feels ridiculous, like I'm pretending. But after a few minutes, something shifts. I actually feel warmer, more present, more connected to the space around me.

Maybe this is what it means to "purge the yuck"- not by analyzing it or understanding it or writing about it, but by physically transforming it through the body.

Jung wrote extensively about dreams as communications from the unconscious. He distinguished between the "personal unconscious" (containing repressed memories and experiences from one's own life) and the "collective unconscious" (containing archetypal patterns shared across all humanity).

This dream feels like it's coming from somewhere deeper than my personal anxiety about the upcoming production. Maybe that's what I'm actually afraid of. Not judgment or criticism or failure, but simply being seen as I actually am.

November 7, 2019 - interlude: on writing

I've been thinking about the relationship between writing this thesis and working on this role. Both are creative processes that require a kind of openness or availability. But they also both trigger my tendency toward self-consciousness and over-analysis.

When I'm writing, I become aware of myself writing. I think about how the words will be received, whether they're interesting or insightful enough, whether I'm revealing too much or not enough. This meta-awareness interrupts the flow, makes the writing feel labored rather than natural.

The same thing happens in acting. I become aware of myself acting. I think about how I look, how I sound, whether I'm making interesting choices. This awareness creates a layer of artifice, a performance of performing.

The solution, in both cases, seems to be the same: stop trying to control how I'm perceived and just focus on what I'm doing. In writing, that means trusting that if I'm honest about my experience, that honesty will be interesting enough. In acting, that means trusting that if I'm fully engaged with the given circumstances of the scene, my performance will be compelling enough.

But this is easier said than done. The habit of self-consciousness is deeply ingrained. It took years to develop and it will take sustained practice to transform.

One thing I've noticed: the writing becomes easier when I give up trying to be impressive. When I stop reaching for the perfectly crafted sentence or the profoundly insightful observation and just say what I'm actually thinking, the words flow more naturally.

Maybe the same will be true in rehearsal. Maybe I need to give up trying to be impressive and just focus on being present with the material.

Rehearsals start in a week. I've been doing the preparatory work: reading and re-reading the script, thinking about Freddie's relationships with Pip and Paul (his partners in the triad), considering what kind of person would choose polyamory as a relationship structure.

She's interested in how desire works, how people navigate jealousy and possessiveness, how love can expand rather than contract.

Freddie is articulate, philosophical, comfortable with his choices. He's not apologetic about his lifestyle but he's also not evangelical about it. He simply lives in a way that feels authentic to him.

Stanislavski famously said "there are no small parts, only small actors." But what he meant by this wasn't about the size of the role- he meant that an actor who is fully present and committed brings depth and truth to any role, while an actor who is distracted or self-conscious diminishes even the most significant role.

Presence, commitment, truth. These are the qualities I need to cultivate. Not cleverness, not technique, not impressive choices. Just presence.

November 14, 2019 - mind as vehicle?

I've been thinking about the metaphor of "mind as vehicle." The idea that consciousness is something we inhabit, something we drive, something we use to navigate the world.

But this metaphor has limitations. It suggests a separation between the driver (the true self) and the vehicle (the mind/body). It implies that there's a "you" who has a mind rather than a "you" who is a mind.

Buddhism challenges this dualistic thinking. In Buddhist philosophy, there is no essential self separate from the aggregates of experience. What we call "I" is actually a constantly changing process rather than a fixed entity. The sense of a separate self is an illusion created by the mind's tendency to grasp and identify.

Applied to acting, this perspective is liberating. If there's no fixed self, then there's nothing to protect. If identity is fluid rather than fixed, then taking on another character isn't a threat to authenticity- it's just another expression of the same fundamental process.

This is what Chekhov might mean by the "higher self"- not a superior version of the ego, but a perspective that transcends ego identification altogether. From this perspective, being Freddie and being Topher aren't contradictory. They're both temporary patterns arising in consciousness.

I'm not sure I can sustain this perspective in practice. It's easy to think about in abstract terms but harder to embody in the rehearsal room when I'm worried about how I look or whether I'm making the right choice.

But maybe that's the practice- noticing when I contract into self-consciousness and gently expanding back into presence. Over and over. That's the work.

November 16, 2019 - center of the world

Today I caught myself in a familiar pattern: thinking that everyone at rehearsal will be watching me, judging me, evaluating my performance. Then I realized: everyone else is thinking the exact same thing about themselves.

This is what psychologists call the "spotlight effect"- the tendency to overestimate how much others are noticing and evaluating us. We assume we're the center of other people's attention because we're the center of our own attention.

But here's the thing: if everyone believes they're the center of attention, then no one actually is. We're all so preoccupied with our own self-consciousness that we barely notice what anyone else is doing.

This should be comforting. It means that most of the judgment I fear is actually happening only in my own mind. The audience isn't sitting there with a critical eye, looking for mistakes. They're hoping to be moved, entertained, transported. They want me to succeed.

Chekhov writes about the "atmosphere" of a scene- the invisible but palpable quality of energy that exists between actors and between actors and audience. He suggests that atmosphere is created collaboratively, that it's not something an individual actor imposes but something that emerges from the collective imagination of everyone in the space.

If this is true, then performance isn't about me at the center being observed by others at the periphery. It's about all of us- actors and audience- creating something together. The center is everywhere and nowhere.

Tomorrow is the first rehearsal. I'm going to try to remember this.

November 17, 2019 - ruining nuance

I've been writing so much lately, trying to analyze and understand and articulate every aspect of my psychological process. But I'm starting to wonder if this is actually helpful or if it's just another form of self-consciousness.

There's a quality of freshness, of discovery, that happens when you encounter something for the first time. But if you analyze it too thoroughly before you've actually experienced it, you flatten the experience into concepts. You lose the nuance, the ambiguity, the alive quality of not-knowing.

This is the danger of this thesis project: that by trying to document my process of preparing for this role, I'm actually interfering with that process. That by writing about stage fright, I'm reinforcing my identity as someone who struggles with stage fright.

Jung warns about this in his writings on the shadow. He says that when we bring unconscious material into consciousness, we must be careful not to concretize it or identify with it. The point isn't to create a more detailed map of the psyche but to expand our capacity to hold complexity without reducing it to fixed categories.

Maybe I need to write less for a while. Maybe I need to stop analyzing and just... rehearse. Just act. Just be present with the material without immediately converting that experience into language.

The first rehearsal is tomorrow. I'm going to try to stay in the experience rather than observing myself having the experience. I'm going to try to trust the process rather than controlling it.

We'll see how that goes.

This is where Chekhov's approach differs from the Stanislavski system. Chekhov would say that the actor doesn't need to remember a time they felt a particular way- they can use their imagination to create that feeling fresh in each moment. The imagination is just as real, just as valid, as memory.

In some ways, this is more challenging. It's easier to rely on a remembered emotion than to generate fresh emotion through imagination. Memory is passive; imagination is active. But imagination also allows for more flexibility, more range, more freedom from the limitations of personal history.

I'm still figuring this out. Still practicing. Still learning.

But now that it's over, I'm not sure what I've proven. I played a role. I did it reasonably well. I experienced moments of genuine presence and moments of self-conscious performance. The audience seemed to connect with the work.

Is that enough? Have I achieved my goal of becoming a better actor?

Chekhov writes: "From the very beginning of his study, he has been inside the character; his reward will be his own inner growth."

Inner growth. Not external validation, not acclaim, not proof of talent. Just the incremental expansion of capacity, the gradual deepening of presence.

Maybe that's what I've gained- not mastery, but a deeper understanding of the practice. Not arrival, but commitment to the journey.

March 13, 2020 - honing attention

Everything has changed.

COVID-19, the coronavirus that started in China and has been spreading globally, has reached the United States in force. The NBA suspended their season. Tom Hanks tested positive. Universities are moving to online classes. Large gatherings are being cancelled.

Yesterday, the Actors' Equity Association (the union representing professional stage actors) issued a statement suspending all rehearsals and performances through at least early April. Theatres across the country are going dark.

SLAC has cancelled the rest of their season. How to Transcend a Happy Marriage had just closed, so we got our full run in. But the next show in the season- the one several of my friends were rehearsing- has been indefinitely postponed.

I should feel lucky that I got to complete my show before this happened. And I do feel lucky. But I also feel disoriented, unmoored. The theatre world I've been part of for years has suddenly been suspended. All the productions that were in development, all the auditions that were scheduled, all the performances that were planned- everything is on hold.

I keep thinking about that moment in the preface where I wrote about audiences as an ocean. Now the ocean has receded. We're standing on the shore watching the water pull back, knowing that something is coming but not knowing what.

My thesis was supposed to conclude with reflection on the performance, synthesis of the acting theory and psychological concepts, some kind of wisdom gleaned from the experience. But how can I synthesize anything when the world has just fundamentally changed?

March 25, 2020 - letting go

Utah is under a "Stay Home, Stay Safe" order. Non-essential businesses are closed. We're supposed to maintain six feet of distance from anyone outside our household. Grocery stores have one-way aisles and capacity limits. It feels dystopian.

I've been trying to write but struggling to focus. Everything I want to say about acting and presence and stage fright feels trivial in the face of a global pandemic. People are dying. Healthcare workers are overwhelmed. The economy is collapsing. And I'm supposed to be analyzing my psychological process in a play that closed two weeks ago?

But maybe that's the point. Maybe the work of being present, of managing fear, of staying connected to what's alive- maybe that work is more relevant now than ever.

The pandemic has revealed something about collective anxiety that resonates with my individual experience of stage fright. That sense of being observed, evaluated, found potentially contaminated- we're all experiencing that now. Every interaction is charged with potential threat. Every cough or sneeze triggers hypervigilance. We're all living in a constant state of performance anxiety, trying to demonstrate that we're safe, we're clean, we're not the danger.

And underneath it all is the fundamental fear that animated my stage fright: the fear of being seen as I actually am, vulnerable and mortal.

Death has always been abstract for me- something that happens to other people, to old people, later. But suddenly it's present. Not immediately threatening to me personally (I'm young and healthy), but present as a real possibility for people I love, for people in my community.

How does an actor prepare to play a role in a world where theatres are closed indefinitely? How does anyone prepare for anything when the future has become so uncertain?

Maybe the only preparation is the practice of presence itself. Being here, now, with what is, rather than what was or what might be.

March 28, 2020 - opening night

That header is a lie. There was no opening night. I'm writing about the opening night of How to Transcend which already happened, which is now a memory, which took place in what already feels like a different era.

But I realize I never actually wrote about opening night itself. I wrote about the first performance, but I didn't capture what it felt like standing in the wings before my first entrance, knowing that months of preparation and decades of longing were about to converge into a single moment of stepping into the light.

What did it feel like?

Terrifying and exhilarating. Familiar and utterly foreign. Like standing at the edge of the ocean, feeling the pull of the tide, knowing you're about to dive in.

I remember thinking: "This is it. This is what I've been preparing for. Not just for this show, but for years. Every acting class, every audition, every performance- it's all been leading to this moment of choosing to be fully present despite the fear."

And then I walked on stage and it wasn't about me anymore. It was about Freddie, about the play, about the story we were telling together. The self-consciousness I had anticipated never fully materialized because I didn't have time for it. The work demanded my attention.

That's what I learned from the whole experience: the cure for self-consciousness isn't trying not to be self-conscious. It's having something more important to focus on.

April 5, 2020 - standstill

A friend from the company, J Todd, killed himself last week.

I didn't know him well. We had done one show together a few years ago. He was talented, funny, kind. He struggled with depression and addiction. The isolation of quarantine must have been unbearable for him.

I keep thinking about how much actors need to be around other actors, how much we need the collaborative energy of rehearsal, the shared experience of performance. When you take that away- when you isolate people who define themselves through connection- something essential gets lost.

Theatre isn't just a job or a hobby for people who do it seriously. It's a way of making sense of the world, a way of processing experience, a way of feeling less alone. Without it, some people don't know how to survive.

The theatre community is grieving. People are posting memories of J Todd on social media, sharing photos from shows he'd been in, expressing shock and sadness. But we can't gather to mourn together. We can't hold each other. We can't stand in a circle and share stories and cry together the way we would normally do.

I didn't know him well enough to claim deep personal grief. But I feel the loss- of his presence, of his potential, of all the performances he would have given if he'd been able to hold on.

Actors are so vulnerable. We expose ourselves emotionally, we seek validation through performance, we struggle with the tension between authentic self-expression and the need for external approval. For many of us, the work itself is therapeutic- it gives us a container for difficult feelings, a way to transform pain into art.

But when you lose access to that container, when all the theatres close and all the auditions are cancelled and there's no foreseeable future for the work- that's when things can become desperate. If an actor's got a play they're working on, even one a year from now, they've got something. If all the theatres shut down, that takes a toll.

I could never assign a reason for J Todd's suicide because I never really knew him. I envisioned offering him a ride back to Herriman on a night when rehearsal had gone late and he'd missed the last train. I imagined him telling me his history, how he went from being an acting student at BYU to landing at American Conservatory Theatre in San Francisco, what it was like to act at Utah Shakes, what about his time in New York, what it feels like to be back in Utah.

That opportunity is gone.

Conclusion

April 14, 2020 - approaching the end

What am I left with at the end of this journey?

In one of my first writings, I had all these ideas for a play or a guidebook for actors or some sort of performance, and instead, I have a collection of my experiences and the thoughts and feelings that went along with them. I fear that I've left the reader with an altogether anxious and depressed jumble, that I haven't offered the reader any solutions or conclusions. The play could have been a victorious ending, but it fell away and was replaced with global catastrophe, loss, and great uncertainty.

The performance is in reality a mutual creation of actors and audience, and the Atmosphere is an irresistible bond between actor and audience, a medium with which the audience can inspire the actors by sending them waves of confidence, understanding, and love. Michael Chekhov, On the Technique of Acting

In my preface, I described the audience as an ocean and connected it with an early trauma. I remain afraid of being observed, absorbed with the possibility of being judged, criticized, or found wanting. Imagining a benevolent audience sending waves of understanding makes my body soften and reminds me of the reasons why I love to act on the stage. I rarely imagine that, but I can remember that more.

I don't know when audiences will be able to gather safely again. The coronavirus has incited the necessity of physical separation, abolished closeness, made us afraid of each other. Even when we are able to gather again, will audiences feel safe enough to enjoy a play?

From the very beginning of his study, he has been inside the character; his reward will be his own inner growth. Michael Chekhov, On the Technique of Acting

Here is the place where I wrap up this thesis. I again sit facing a shifting mass of possibilities, but they look nothing like I could have predicted. I don't know when people will feel safe sitting in a theatre together. The idea of an audience has dissolved and dispersed. I will return to this section over the coming weeks and try to say something that feels like an ending.

April 21, 2020 - results & discussion

Despite the upside-down ending to this narrative, I feel that I have captured succinctly the psychology of being myself as an actor and harvested the thoughts and feelings that made up my preparatory process in the face of rehearsal and performance.

The transformations I have undergone over the course of this project include:

  • my capacity for and appreciation of ambiguity and ambivalence, which has increased
  • my relationship with the work of Sarah Ruhl, which has deepened and veered
  • my relationship with my ability to write and to act, which has softened and widened
  • the length of my hair, which is now longer than it's ever been
  • the length of this thesis, which is the largest piece of writing I've ever written
  • my appreciation for the possibility of theatrical events, which has begun to ache
  • my relationship to the inevitability of death and the myriad means by which it can happen, which became starker and more present suddenly and unexpectedly
  • a sense of ownership of sensations and impulses arising in my body, which unfolded

What I didn't do, that perhaps I'll have the resources and capacity to do in the future, is write anything about the changes in my thinking and being that have occurred thanks to the Jungian Analysts from whom I have learned a great deal, because I feel I am still in an embryonic phase of my understanding of psychology. Perhaps, as my development continues, I will be fortunate enough to enter a path of study centered on true Jungian Analysis and embark on my own analytic process.

Another thing I look forward to exploring further (and perhaps even writing about) is the Michael Chekhov technique, which lent me its credibility when I lost a sense of what an actor should do. Perhaps, once some kind of safety arrives on the planet, I will be fortunate enough to attend the Michael Chekhov School for further training and exploration.

How to Transcend happens next spring. The work I have done on my role, some of which I hope is represented here in this writing, will continue. I will continue to transform. And, I'm sure, more great uncertainty is on its way.

I am currently experiencing a glowing sort of spacious warmth as I type these words, thinking back on the work I have done and the work I will continue to do.

I tried to tell the truth.

I know it wasn't science but I hope it held some value to you.
